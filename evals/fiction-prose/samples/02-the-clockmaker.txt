When the last customer had gone, Vesna locked the door and let the shop fall quiet around her. It was never truly quiet—there were forty clocks on the walls and each kept its own opinion of the hour—but the human noise was gone, and that was close enough.

She sat at the bench with the small brass movement open before her, its gears no bigger than seeds, and waited for her hands to remember what her eyes could barely follow. The trick was not to look too hard. The trick, her father had told her, was to let the work come up through the fingers.

The clock had belonged to a woman who had died in the spring. The daughter had brought it in wrapped in a tea towel, apologizing for the state of it, and Vesna had taken it the way she took all of them—without promises, only the quiet certainty that she would try.

A spring had broken. That was all. A small thing, a cheap thing, and yet the whole mechanism had stopped because of it, the hands frozen at twenty past four on some ordinary afternoon that the dead woman had not known would be her last recorded hour.

Vesna knew that feeling. She fitted the new spring with a breath held behind her teeth, and felt the movement take it, and felt the faint shiver run through the brass as the escapement caught and held and began, very softly, to count again.

Outside, the light had gone amber against the windows. She wound the clock and set it among the others, and for a moment all forty of them seemed to lean toward the same minute. Then they scattered back into disagreement, and Vesna smiled, and reached for her coat.
