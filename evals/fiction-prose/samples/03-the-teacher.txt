By the second week of term, Mr. Adeyemi had learned all their names, and most of their silences. The names were the easy part. The silences took longer—the boy by the window who knew the answer and would not give it, the girl who answered everything because the quiet frightened her, the small one at the back who had not yet decided whether the room was safe.

It was the small one he watched. Daniel. He came in each morning with his coat still buttoned to the throat, as if he might need to leave at any moment, and he sat with his hands flat on the desk and waited to be told what to want.

Adeyemi did not push. He had pushed, once, early in his career, and he had learned what pushing cost. Instead he left a book on the corner of Daniel's desk—not handed, not assigned, only left, the way you leave bread out for a bird that does not yet trust the window.

For three days the book did not move. On the fourth, it was open. On the fifth, Daniel looked up in the middle of a lesson and asked a question—small, careful, but his own—and the light that came into the room then had nothing to do with the grey morning at the glass.

"Good question," Adeyemi said, and moved on quickly, because he knew that too much attention could close a door as surely as too little.

At the end of the day he stood among the empty desks and the settling dust, and listened to the building tick and cool around him. There were worse things, he thought, than to be the one who waited.
