By the time the light came on, the fog had already taken the harbor. Halloran stood at the window with his hands wrapped around a cup that had gone cold an hour ago, and watched the grey close over the water the way it always did in October—slow, patient, certain of itself.

He knew the boats were still out there. He could not see them, but he knew, the way he knew the stairs in the dark, the way he knew the weight of the lamp before he lifted it. The knowing was older than the seeing.

"You'll wear a hole in that floor," his wife said from the doorway. She had brought more coffee, though he had not asked.

"They went out late," he said. "The Donnelly boat. The young one."

She did not answer. There was nothing to answer. The light turned, and turned, and laid its long arm across the fog, and somewhere under it the boats would find the channel or they would not.

Morning came thin and reluctant. The fog lifted by inches, and the harbor gave itself back—the masts first, then the hulls, then the wet stones of the breakwater where the gulls had already gathered. Halloran counted the boats as they came in. He counted them twice.

The Donnelly boat was the last. It rounded the point low in the water, heavy with the night's catch, and the young one at the tiller lifted a hand toward the tower without looking up, as if he had known all along that someone was counting.

Halloran lifted his hand in return. Then he climbed the stairs to put the light to sleep, his knees complaining on every step, and thought that there were worse things to be than the man who waited.
