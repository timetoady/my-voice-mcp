The river was always highest in the hour before dawn, when the tide turned and pushed the dark water back against the current, and that was when Marko liked it best. The crossings were empty then. Just him, and the engine's low complaint, and the far bank coming and going in the mist.

He had run the ferry for thirty years. His father had run it before him, and the cable was the same cable, splice over splice, so that no single strand of it was older than a decade and yet the whole of it was older than Marko himself. He thought about that sometimes, mid-river, with the town asleep behind him and the hills not yet awake ahead.

A woman waited at the landing. He had not expected anyone. She stood with a single bag at her feet and her coat pulled close, and she did not wave or call—she only watched the ferry come, the way people watched when they were leaving something rather than going toward it.

"Early," he said, as she stepped aboard.

"Couldn't sleep," she said, and that was all, and he asked nothing more. The river asked the questions on a crossing like this. A man only had to be quiet enough to let it.

They went out into the mist. The light was coming now, grey at first and then the faint gold of it on the water, and the woman stood at the rail with her hands tight on the cold metal and her face turned to the far bank. Whatever she was leaving, she did not look back at it once.

Marko brought her in gentle against the landing. She paid, and thanked him, and walked up into the waking town. He turned the ferry around and went back for whoever else the morning might bring.
